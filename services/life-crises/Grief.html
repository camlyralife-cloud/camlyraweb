Frequently asked questions

Grief counselling and supportive therapies like Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT) are commonly used to help individuals process loss. These approaches focus on understanding emotions, coping with pain, and gradually adjusting to life after loss.

Grief counselling is a form of therapy that supports individuals in coping with the emotional, psychological, and practical challenges that come after a loss. It provides a safe space to express feelings and begin the healing process.

A grief counsellor helps individuals navigate their loss by offering emotional support, guidance, and coping strategies. They assist in processing grief, managing difficult emotions, and adapting to life changes in a healthy way.

There is no single “hardest” stage, as grief is deeply personal and varies for each individual. However, stages like depression or acceptance can feel particularly challenging as individuals process the reality of loss and adjust to change.

What is Grief?

Grief is the deep emotional pain and sorrow experienced after a significant loss, most commonly the death of a loved one. It is a natural and personal process that affects emotional, physical, and mental well-being.

Everyone experiences grief differently. While it can feel overwhelming, it is a normal response to loss and healing takes time.

Types of Grief

  • Uncomplicated Grief: Gradually improves over time.
  • Anticipatory Grief: Grief before an expected loss.
  • Delayed Grief: Emotions appear later.
  • Complicated Grief: Persistent and intense grief.
  • Traumatic Grief: Linked to sudden or distressing loss.
  • Collective Grief: Shared loss within a group or community.

Stages of Grief

  • Denial: Shock or disbelief
  • Anger: Frustration or helplessness
  • Bargaining: “What if” or “If only” thoughts
  • Depression: Deep sadness and withdrawal
  • Acceptance: Adjusting to the loss

Common Symptoms

  • Intense sadness, longing, or guilt
  • Confusion or difficulty concentrating
  • Fatigue and low energy
  • Changes in sleep or appetite
  • Physical discomfort (headaches, nausea)
  • Withdrawal from social activities

Causes of Grief

  • Death of a loved one or pet
  • End of a relationship or marriage
  • Loss of job or financial stability
  • Health issues or disability
  • Major life transitions or changes

Ways to Cope

  • Allow yourself to feel and process emotions
  • Talk to trusted friends or family
  • Maintain basic self-care (sleep, food, movement)
  • Return to activities you enjoy gradually
  • Join support groups for shared understanding
  • Seek professional help if needed

Supporting Someone in Grief

  • Be present and listen without judgment
  • Offer practical help (meals, errands)
  • Encourage open expression of feelings
  • Avoid minimizing their loss
  • Be patient with their healing process
