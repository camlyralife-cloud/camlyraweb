Frequently asked questions

Trauma is an emotional and psychological response to a deeply distressing or disturbing experience. It can result from events like accidents, loss, abuse, or any situation that overwhelms a person’s ability to cope.

Common signs include anxiety, flashbacks, nightmares, emotional numbness, irritability, difficulty concentrating, and avoidance of reminders of the event. Physical symptoms like fatigue or sleep issues may also occur.

Trauma can be treated through therapies such as Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT), Trauma-Focused CBT, and EMDR. These approaches help individuals process their experiences, reduce distress, and build healthy coping mechanisms.

Many individuals experience significant healing and improvement with the right support and therapy. While the memory of trauma may remain, its emotional impact can be reduced, allowing individuals to lead healthy and fulfilling lives.

What is Trauma?

Trauma is an emotional response to a deeply distressing or disturbing event. It can occur after experiences such as accidents, violence, loss, or any situation that feels overwhelming or threatening.

Trauma affects people differently. It can impact emotional, physical, and psychological well-being, and in some cases may develop into conditions like post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD).

Types of Trauma

  • Acute Trauma: Resulting from a single distressing event.
  • Chronic Trauma: Repeated or prolonged exposure to stress.
  • Complex Trauma: Exposure to multiple traumatic events.
  • Secondary Trauma: Experiencing trauma through close contact with someone affected.

Common Symptoms

  • Shock, denial, or confusion
  • Fear, anxiety, or emotional overwhelm
  • Difficulty concentrating
  • Sleep disturbances or fatigue
  • Physical symptoms like headaches or tension
  • Withdrawal from people or activities

Risk Factors

  • Previous traumatic experiences
  • Lack of support systems
  • Existing mental health conditions
  • High levels of stress
  • Exposure to ongoing or severe events

Impact

  • Emotional distress and mood changes
  • Difficulty in relationships
  • Reduced ability to cope with stress
  • Physical health issues
  • Increased risk of anxiety or depression

Ways to Cope

  • Stay connected with trusted people
  • Maintain daily routines
  • Engage in calming activities or hobbies
  • Practice grounding and breathing techniques
  • Take care of your physical health
  • Seek professional support when needed

When to Seek Help

  • If symptoms persist or worsen over time
  • If daily functioning becomes difficult
  • If you feel overwhelmed or unable to cope
  • If you have distressing thoughts or emotions
  • Reach out to a mental health professional or helpline
