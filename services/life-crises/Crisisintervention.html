Frequently asked questions

Crisis counselling is a short-term, immediate form of support provided to individuals facing intense emotional distress due to a traumatic or overwhelming situation. It focuses on stabilizing emotions, ensuring safety, and helping the person regain a sense of control.

The main goals of crisis intervention are to ensure immediate safety, reduce emotional distress, restore functioning, and help individuals develop coping strategies. It also aims to prevent long-term psychological impact by providing timely support.

The five stages of crisis typically include the initial impact, escalation of stress, loss of coping ability, peak crisis state, and recovery or breakdown. These stages reflect how a person responds emotionally and psychologically to a crisis situation.

The best way to handle a crisis is to stay calm, ensure safety, seek immediate support, and focus on one step at a time. Reaching out to professionals, trusted individuals, or support services can help manage the situation effectively.

What is Crisis Intervention?

Crisis intervention refers to immediate, short-term support provided to individuals experiencing intense emotional, mental, or behavioral distress. It focuses on stabilizing the situation and helping individuals regain control and coping ability.

A crisis occurs when a person feels overwhelmed and unable to cope with a situation using their usual problem-solving skills. Early support can prevent long-term psychological harm.

Examples of Crisis

  • Natural disasters (earthquakes, floods)
  • Loss of a loved one
  • Accidents or life-threatening situations
  • Violence or assault
  • Severe emotional distress or suicidal thoughts

Types of Intervention

  • Individual: One-on-one emotional support and coping strategies
  • Family: Addressing crisis within family dynamics
  • Group: Support for people facing similar crises
  • Emergency: Immediate response to critical situations
  • Crisis Counselling: Short-term therapeutic support

Common Symptoms

  • Intense anxiety, panic, or sadness
  • Confusion or difficulty making decisions
  • Withdrawal or isolation
  • Anger or aggressive behavior
  • Sleep or appetite changes
  • Fatigue or physical tension

Causes

  • Trauma or abuse
  • Loss or grief
  • Major life changes (job loss, divorce)
  • Mental health conditions
  • Substance use or withdrawal
  • Medical emergencies or accidents

Goals of Intervention

  • Stabilize emotional distress
  • Ensure immediate safety
  • Provide emotional support
  • Restore coping abilities
  • Connect to ongoing care and resources

Tips to Cope

  • Create a personal safety plan
  • Maintain daily routine when possible
  • Talk to trusted people
  • Practice grounding and breathing techniques
  • Focus on small, manageable steps
  • Seek professional help when needed
